Monsoon & Water Conservation

Why every raindrop counts — and what you can do about it

← Back to all issues

Welcome to the first issue of The Green Gene! Every quarter we cover one environmental theme with news, tips, a quiz, and contributions from classmates. This time: monsoon and water. It hits close to home for those of us in Patna — we see both the floods and the dry months, which makes water conservation something we actually need to think about.

— Ayushman, Editor

News Alert

What's happening with water this monsoon?

India receives about 75% of its annual rainfall during the monsoon season (June–September). But the paradox is that while floods affect millions every year, groundwater levels in most cities including Patna keep falling due to over-extraction and poor storage infrastructure.

Did you know?

India has only 4% of the world's freshwater but supports 18% of the global population. Per person, that's a severe shortage.

Rainwater harvesting — collecting and storing monsoon water for later use — is one of the simplest solutions available at both household and school level. Several Delhi schools have already installed systems that cut their water bills by over 30%.

Try at Home

5 water habits to start this week

  • 💧 Turn off the tap while brushing — saves up to 12 litres per minute.
  • 🪣 Keep a bucket under the shower while it warms up. Use that water for plants.
  • 🌧️ Put a container outside during rain. Even a small bucket gives you free water for plants for days.
  • 🍽️ Wash vegetables in a bowl, not under running water. Reuse that water for mopping.
  • 🔧 Report a dripping tap at home. A tap dripping once per second wastes 11,000 litres a year.

Quiz

Test your water knowledge

1. What percentage of Earth's water is freshwater?

Correct! Only about 3% of Earth's water is freshwater — and most of that is locked in glaciers.

2. Which Indian city was the first to make rainwater harvesting compulsory?

Correct! Chennai made rainwater harvesting compulsory in 2003 after severe water shortages.

3. How much water does a 5-minute shower use on average?

Correct! A typical shower uses 60 litres in 5 minutes. Switching to a bucket can cut that to 15–20 litres.
